Fun Facts



• Belugas are opportunistic feeders, meaning they eat a wide variety of prey depending on what’s available. They use echolocation—a biological sonar system—to locate prey even in murky waters or under thick ice!

• Adult belugas are known for their pure white coloration, which helps them blend into icy environments. However, calves are born grey and gradually lighten over their first few years of life, typically reaching full white coloration by the age of five.

• Unlike many other whales, belugas lack a dorsal fin. This adaptation makes it easier for them to swim under ice without getting snagged or injured. Another unique feature is their flexible neck, allowing them to move their heads independently—useful for navigating and hunting in tight spaces. Their large, rounded forehead, called a melon, can change shape to aid both in communication and fine-tuning their echolocation abilities.

• Beluga whales are classified as Near Threatened by the IUCN (International Union for Conservation of Nature), though some regional populations are considered endangered. They face several threats, including climate change, habitat loss, pollution, and hunting in certain regions. Conservation efforts are vital to ensure their survival.

• Belugas are often called the "canaries of the sea" due to their wide range of vocalizations, which include clicks, whistles, and chirps. They use these sounds for communication with one another and for navigating their environment.

• They are highly social animals, living in groups called pods that usually consist of 10–20 individuals. These pods can merge, forming superpods of several hundred whales, especially during migrations or in areas with abundant food.

• Unlike many other whale species, belugas are comfortable in both saltwater and freshwater environments. Some belugas travel far upriver, swimming hundreds of kilometers inland, which is rare behavior for marine mammals.

• Beluga whales have been observed blowing bubbles, possibly for play or communication. They are also known for their curious and playful nature, often approaching boats and interacting with humans.

• Belugas can hold their breath for up to 25 minutes during deep dives. However, they usually dive to more moderate depths of around 20–40 meters when searching for food.

• The lifespan of a beluga whale in the wild is typically 30 to 50 years. However, threats like pollution and climate change can affect their longevity.





where they live



Beluga whales are well-adapted to life in cold waters, with their iconic milky white coloration helping them blend seamlessly into the icy Arctic environment. They inhabit regions throughout the Arctic Ocean and the sub-Arctic seas, including parts of the Beaufort Sea, Bering Sea, Chukchi Sea, and Hudson Bay.

Coastal and Shallow Waters Belugas typically prefer shallow coastal areas, which provide both safety from predators and abundant feeding opportunities. They are often found near inlets, bays, and estuaries, where food sources such as fish, squid, and crustaceans thrive.

Summer Migrations During the warmer summer months, beluga whales migrate to river mouths and estuaries, including the St. Lawrence River in Canada and the Amur River in Russia. These areas offer warmer, brackish waters, where they molt (shed their skin) and raise their calves. Some groups travel hundreds of kilometers inland, making belugas unique among whales for their tolerance to freshwater.

Winter Range In winter, Beluga whales may move to deeper offshore waters to avoid ice trapping. However, they are known for their ability to navigate under pack ice, using cracks or leads (openings) in the ice to breathe.

Regional Populations Canadian Arctic: Found near the Hudson Bay, Cumberland Sound, and the Mackenzie Delta. Russian Arctic: Populations thrive in the White, Kara, and Barents Seas. Alaska: Belugas inhabit Cook Inlet and the Beaufort and Bering Seas. Beluga whales' ability to live in diverse environments—ranging from saltwater oceans to freshwater rivers—highlights their remarkable adaptability. However, their habitat is increasingly affected by climate change, which alters ice patterns and impacts food availability, posing a growing threat to these fascinating creatures.







what they eat



Beluga whales are carnivores, meaning their diet consists entirely of meat. They feed on a variety of fish, such as salmon, herring, cod, and smelt. Their food preferences can vary based on the season and availability, with fish playing a crucial role during the summer months when they migrate to coastal waters.

In addition to fish, belugas consume squid, octopuses, and marine worms, as well as small crustaceans like shrimp, crabs, and amphipods. These opportunistic feeders hunt in both shallow coastal areas and deeper waters, adjusting their diet according to what’s accessible in their habitat.

Belugas use their highly developed echolocation abilities to locate prey in murky waters or under ice. Once prey is found, they rely on their flexible neck and powerful jaws to grasp and swallow it whole, as they don’t have teeth designed for chewing.

Due to their varied diet, belugas play a vital role in maintaining the balance of marine ecosystems. However, environmental changes that affect the abundance of their prey—such as overfishing or rising water temperatures—can threaten the availability of food, impacting their health and survival.
