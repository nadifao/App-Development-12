Beluga Whale

Beluga Whales are known for their milky white colouration, allowing them to blend into their Arctic and sub-Arctic environments.
They inhabit the Arctic Ocean and adjoining seas, often found in shallow coastal waters.
They are also known to migrate to estuaries and rivers during summer months, tolerating brackish water.

Beluga whales are carnivores, feeding on a variety of fish (such as salmon and herring), squid, shrimp, and other crustaceans.
They are opportunistic feeders, using echolocation to locate prey even in murky waters!



Adults are pure white, while calves are born grey and gradually lighten over the first few years. Belugas lack a dorsal fin, making it easier for them to swim under ice. They have a flexible neck and large forehead (melon), which can change shape to aid communication and echolocation.


Beluga whales are classified as Near Threatened by the IUCN, with some populations considered endangered. They are threatened by climate change, habitat loss, pollution, and hunting in certain regions.
